U.K. universities tuition fees are frequently a source of controversy, and came into limelight as the Telegraph wrote about the Russell Group “profiting from students’ misery” during the pandemic.

The Russell Group, which consists of twenty-four universities, are public research universities in the United Kingdom. The Russell Group is the equivalent of the American Ivy League of prestigious universities, according to The Guardian. These universities are highly ranked and provide world-leading research opportunities as they are awarded two thirds of all U.K. research grants and contract income.

Most universities in the U.K. offer shorter programs compared to countries such as the U.S. - three years for the average undergraduate degree instead of four, and one year for a master’s degree instead of two.

According to surveys, Russell Group graduates found work faster than their peers elsewhere as they were highly sought after by employers. Most universities in the U.K. are government financed. But where do you think universities get most of their income?

The Subheading of the Income Source Part

Russell Group Universities have become more reliant on students’ payments to operate than on public spending. While funds from government bodies have also increased during the past decade, their share in the income balance has shrunk and it has been overtaken by tuition fees. In the year 2020-2021, 13 out of 24 Russell Group universities received more than 50% of their income from tuition fees.

This is a trend that the sector as a whole has seen grow since 2010, when student payments surpassed government funds for the first time, the Higher Education Statistics Agency (HESA) said in an email. This was two years before the policy change that tripled tuition fees for domestic students from £3,000 to £9,000. Since then, the economic relevance of school fees only continued to swell to the point of accounting for more than half of universities’ income in 2016-17.

The question of who should foot the bill for higher education is still controversial. Should attending university be a mostly self-funded career choice or should taxpayers invest more in training professionals? Several studies show that tertiary education is a major driver of economic competitiveness, and the UK is in need of highly qualified labour. Last year, British employers had to recruit 239,987 professionals overseas and sponsor them using the skilled worker visa scheme— a 33% jump from 2019.

Many argue that the government has continued to chip in through student finance programmes, as a HESA spokesperson says: “Nowadays, most universities’ money comes from tuition fees but those tuition fees come often from students who then take a loan from the government to pay for them.” Only England, around 1.5 million students borrow a total of almost £20 billion every year, according to the Parliament latest figures.

“Because the student loans are government-backed and written off after 30 years, students aren’t really shouldering really as much of the cost as it may look,” says Alexis Brown, director of policy and advocacy at the Higher Education Policy Institute (HEPI). “The taxpayer pays for it so it’s not as quite clear cut as it would be if we were in America, where the student actually does pay it all back.”

Only one in four students who started full-time undergraduate degrees in 2020-21 is expected to repay their debt in full, according to forecasts by the Department of Education. This has resulted in the debate of who should bear the economic bulk of higher education to focus more on the design of student loans rather than the affordability of tuition fees.

But not everyone is willing to take a loan to go to university. Even if Brown insists that student loans make it possible for everyone in the UK to go to university, the idea of having a growing debt of thousands of pounds for 30 years can be a deterrent.

International Students: The Willing Cash Cow

“All they’ve been teaching now is theoretical stuff, and they don't even talk about why we are talking about the theories. It's just quite “watery (水, with low difficulty, low value)” anyway. I never met the teacher offline for the three courses we had in the business school last semester…And the classes were just videos recorded a few years ago…It's one teacher for a few hundred students. I think it's quite unfair.” Yijing Sun, a Chinese student studying MSc Financial Economics at Cardiff University, told us.

But Like many other international students, she’s willing to choose to come to the UK for her education. “I think it was worth the money. Because when I first came here, I didn't intend to learn anything, after all, you don't learn much in a year. I believe the main thing is to get some experience.”

The number of students paying international tuition fees (non-EU international students enrolled before 2022), at the 24 Russell Group universities is rising sharply between the financial year ending 2017 and 2021, with 16 universities experiencing an increase of more than 20%, including the University of Glasgow, which has seen a 100.6% increase in non-EU international students. This is closely followed by Queen's University Belfast, which has seen an 81.2% increase.

And these increases were not driven solely by growth in total student numbers, as 21 of them also saw increases in the ratio of non-EU international students numbers to total student numbers. And among these, UCL and the University of Glasgow rose by almost 0.1, which means that for every 10 students, there is 1 more student paying international tuition fees and 1 less student paying local tuition fees. In terms of percentage increase of the ratio, the highest is at Queen's University Belfast at 70%, followed by Glasgow University at 55%.

In its Spending Review 2021, the Russell Group says that "income from international student fees" is "a key area of university activity which generates a surplus". This claim is supported by the Office for Students (OfS) ' The Transparent Approach to Costing (TRAC) 2019-20 Report. Its figures show that while universities across the UK made huge deficits in public-funded teaching and research that year, "Non-publicly funded teaching (primarily overseas students) continued to generate a significant surplus" of 2.32billion.

Another report mentioned by Spending Review 2021 also supports the fact of its reliance on international student fee income. For home-fee students, they "estimate that across the sector deficits average £1,940 per student per year". And “even the most inexpensive classroom-based subjects face deficits of £1,000 per student”.

They say these figures are based on the Development of the OfS's approach to funding published in September 2020 and other data. The paper states that FTE students (Home and EU full-time equivalent students) are causing deficits in five different price groups of education programs. However, only one of these price groups has an average annual cost of over £12,000, and unlike the average home student tuition fee, which is under £9,000, international students' fees are well above this level, typically starting at 20,000 a year, making it a lucrative deal. And this is how they had “been doing more with less” since 2015, as a spokesperson for Universities UK suggested in an interview with the Guardian.

The growth of international students is only possible if the school is willing to recruit, but also if the students are willing to come. In the academic year 2020-2021 (which corresponds to the financial year ending 2021), the largest number of international students coming to the UK are from China at 99,160, followed by the European Union at 66,680 and India in third place at 53,015. The number of students from the EU has remained stable. However, compared to the 2016-2017 academic year, the number of students from China has increased by 48% and the number of students from India has jumped by 430%.

The quality of education is high, the reputation is high, the length of study is short and the total cost is low compared to other English speaking countries such as North America. These are the reasons why large numbers of new middle-class Chinese and Indian students come to study in the UK. However, one of the reasons they come to the UK to attend university may come as a surprise - it is much less difficult to gain admission.

China and India are the top two countries in the world in terms of population, quality education resources per capita are inevitably scarce. Entrance exams like Common Admission Test (CAT), IIT-JEE (Indian Institute of Technology Joint Entrance Examination), Gaokao (China National College Entrance Examination) and Kaoyan (exam to select postgraduates in China), among others, keep getting tougher to crack for students applying in their universities. The number of candidate seats remains the same, while the number of applicants exponentially increases with every passing year. The admissions process for prestigious government universities, in particular, is made more difficult due to the rigorous pattern of the entrance exams.

As Mengyang Li, a Chinese student studying MA History at the University of Exeter told us “...and as long as you pass the application, you don't have to take exams under pressure like in China.” And Yijing Sun said “The reason was very realistic. I was too lazy to work on the exams to go to graduate school in China.”

Student agencies further take the hassle out of the application process for potential international students. Both Chinese students interviewed said they paid 10,000 RMB, or about £1,200 or more, for their services. However, there are many agents who do not charge students and make their profits from the commissions given to them by UK universities. "It's usually 15-25% of the tuition fee," an owner of one such agency, who declined to reveal her personal details, told us. This statement coincides with an article published by HEPI.

For this, we sent FOIs to all 24 Russell Group universities, requesting data on which student agencies they work with and how much they pay in commissions to these student agents. Of these, Oxbridge and Imperial said they do not use student agencies for recruitment, and LSE said it had recruited only 14 students through this channel in the 2020-21 academic year. A further 19 universities said in different ways that "in a highly competitive business environment, publishing this data would put them at a competitive disadvantage" and declined to disclose the figures.

Only the University of York gave us a meaningful response, “The total spend on external recruitment agencies for the 2020/1 financial year was £8,742,094.”

And the money, for the Universities, was well spent.

The Subheading of the Surplus Part

The Operating surplus of universities has always been a contentious subject. As universities have charitable organisation status, they are exempt from paying tax, however, due to this, they are non-profit organisations. Operating surplus is defined as total income minus total expenditure and is therefore essentially profit under a different name. We calculated the Operating Surplus of all Russel group universities using data from the HESA and found in 2020 they amassed (£2,531,400) and in 2021 (£1,074,438) as shown in fig 1.

It is no surprise that the Russel group has come under fire lately for their reported total surplus. The Telegraph’s article titled ‘Russell Group universities “profiting from students” misery’ after amassing £2.2bn cash surplus’, suggests throughout the covid-19 pandemic, Russel group universities have done little in the way of supporting students despite evidently having the financial means to do so. There was no Russel group-wide refunding of tuition fees, alleviation of accommodation costs or bursaries given to students during this time despite an industry-wide reduction in face-to-face teaching. In response to the Telegraph’s article, a Russell Group spokesperson stated universities had “worked hard to prioritise students and provide the best possible experience during the pandemic”—including by investing significantly in digital learning and increasing wider support services.” As well as Russel group surplus demonstrating their strong financial position Fig 2 demonstrates that in the financial year-end 2020-21 staff costs were down from previous years. Equally, figure 2 shows that total expenditure was reduced for the same financial year, likely contributing to the huge operating and total surplus seen. This income and expenditure pattern brings into question the universities teaching provisions during the pandemic.

In the 2021 Student Academic Experience Survey, published by the Higher Education Policy Institute “Forty-four per cent of students report ‘poor or very poor’ value – a significant leap from the 29% of students with that perception in 2019”. So it is clear that many students did not feel online teaching was a valuable use of their tuition fees. In a Guardian article Andreas Schleicher, director of education and skills for the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development stated “I don’t think you can ask students to pay £9,000 for just online lessons.” Scheicher further stated a continuation of online learning post-pandemic would “erode public support for tuition fees of up to £9,259”. In an interview, Andrew Norton, a professor in the Practice of Higher Education Policy at the Australian National University argued “demand for higher education is really not as sensitive to the economic incentives, as you might expect”, and while there is often short term reductions in demand for higher education when changes such as increases in tuition fees occur. The Demand for higher education tends to resume “this very long term trend towards greater participation in our education”. It is safe to assume a similar trend in spite of the type of education provided to students. Whether these factors will reduce numbers of university applicants is up for contention but it is evident that the majority of students prefer to receive in-person teaching. While during the pandemic universities had little choice but to provide students with online classes, they should have at least refunded students a portion of their tuition fees, a sentiment echoed in a telegraph article by Robert Halfon, the Tory chair of the education select committee, stating, “The higher education regulator should work with universities to ensure “students get the money back they deserve”.

University Representatives

In an interview with an owner of an University representative company based in Beijing, we found out really interesting insights on how much universities pay agencies to recruit students, an approximate number of students recruited from these agencies and the percentage of students that apply through these agencies, among other things.

Q: What do you do?

A: I am the owner of our company. We are an exclusive university representative and have over 30 employees. We are not a student agency. Another part of our business is the high-end study abroad application (applying for world-top universities), helping students with systematic CV packaging. The top 50 universities in the world don't really have a shortage of students, they don't use agencies.

Q:What is a university representative?

A:We are responsible for the admissions of a North American university in mainland China and they give us commissions. It’s usually 15-25% of the tuition fee. The worse the university the higher the commission, by the way. It's an open secret. But we also give a percentage of that money to the student agency. We are really more like a regional distributor for an international brand.

Q: So every agency gets money back when they recruit a student for the university?

A:No, the agency has to have a relationship with the university in order to get the money back, for example, if they bypass us as a distributor in China, they can't get the commission. But I'm not sure about the UK.

Q:But I can't see who is their representative and who is their agency in China from the public information of the university. The information is vague.

A:This information is generally only known to agents, not students. The agents are very wary of students bypassing them or going directly to the university representative. University representatives are very close to the university and know everything about the university. They can tell student agencies how likely a student is to get a place. University representatives are actually willing to hand over the job of communicating with students to agents. So that we can maintain the entire China intake with a few staff ourselves. Our relationship with the agencies is like that of a regional distributor and a wholesaler. At our best, we recruited over 200 students to the university in one year.

Q: Do you also do business in the UK?

A: Yes, the UK business is quite developed so we also take part. But we do more on the US side because the UK is a country with a clear hierarchy, where people are divided into classes. There are also classes of degrees, right? They like standard figures, there are fewer things we can do. Whereas with North American applications we have more scope to work with. They are much more demanding in terms of paperwork. Soft skills can play a big role.

Q:What can you do to help students?

A:For students who may or may not be accepted by universities, we are more useful. We have some influence on the school and we know what the school values. We can then allow students to make up for it in a targeted way. It's also good to know if a major is already full, to avoid wasting energy. But it's still difficult for an agent to recommend someone directly to a school. It doesn't usually happen. Unless it's an extremely close personal relationship.

Q: I've heard that schools will run programs specifically for Chinese students in order to make money. Is that true?

A: Of course. I know of a university where the vice-chancellor is Chinese. They offer programmes for Chinese students purely to make money. It's mainly focused on business, MBA. And even the curriculums are fixed, so you can't take classes that you could take in other normal programmes.

Q: Has the epidemic had an impact on your business?

A: Yes. We used to get around 1,000 applications a year, but now the number of applications has dropped significantly. The media has had a big impact on parents. The main thing now is that the country's bilateral relations are not good and there is a fear that children will be at risk if they go there. There have been a particularly high number of children going to high school in the US these past few years who have returned home to take the GaoKao (Chinese University entrance exams). Of course, the universities at the top may be fine, but the ones below them are having a hard time. I guess that will be the case for at least five years.

Q:What do you think is the value of representatives and agencies?

A:It's interesting to say. Chinese parents are more trusting of agents who take money. If you say you can apply for free and only take a commission from the university, they are not comfortable with that. And all agencies have their own admissions channels. This is something that universities and university representatives can't penetrate with advertising. For example, some agencies have private relationships with high schools in China, so they can go directly to the high schools to recruit students. And also, especially for high-end applications, the role of agents cannot be ignored. You need a lot of experience to be able to deal with that.

Q:What percentage of Chinese students who apply to study in the UK do so through an agent?

A:In my experience, the probability of Chinese students applying on their own for undergraduate studies in the UK is basically zero. For postgraduate students, most of them go to agents, 90%.

Most older and public universities use the date Aug. 1 to July 31 for their financial year, that's their accounting year, and so those ones are published. So, the latest data we publish for most public universities is that of the 2019-20 academic year.

Q: I can see that in the key financial indicators page of the data set you provided shows that some higher education providers saw significant cost adjustments in both 2019 and 2020 relating to the accounting for the value of pension schemes, and this is a big part of the movement. What exactly does this movement mean and why is it such a huge part? And why has it driven the whole surplus and deficit up and down?

A: Universities as employers pay into a pension fund or their employees. So, when employees retire, they can withdraw a pension. Now the value of that fund causes most universities to use the same pension scheme. One is that it’s affected by the market by the stock market. What is expected to be the amount of money available to pay future pension depends on changes in the stock market.

So, when that changes the accountants then have to reconsider the future value of that pension scheme and when accountants and the actuaries calculate how much money they'll be able to pay out in the pension they will say whether the universities need to put more money into that to top it up, to make it enough to pay those future pensions.

Well, there's been a lot of changes in stock market prices in recent years, and the value of pension schemes became lower than would have been enough to pay out the pension. So, the universities have had to add more than they expected to that pension account to ensure that future pensions are paid. But they've just done this once and put it in their accounts over two years and it's not expected to be a repeated regular top up payment. But the different universities have accounted for that in different ways and across universities it has perturbed the normal trends in their income and expenditure and their surplus and deficit figures for those years because of that exceptional payment.

Q: Our research findings from the Higher Education Statistics Agency demonstrate that university income has become more reliant on tuition fees in the UK than ever before. A: I think there are two reasons. One is about a decade ago England abolished subsidies for most undergraduate courses and switched to the 9 thousand pound model so that meant the fee income from English students went up significantly. Over this same time period, with the exception of a dip during covid, the number of international students in the UK has gone up and they typically pay much higher fees than the local students. And that has a big effect on the overall share of university revenue that is coming from tuition fees. So I think they're both domestic and international factors here.

Q: Is it a similar picture in Australia?
